The history behind "Joshua Fit the Battle of Jericho" reveals that it is much more than just a retelling of a Bible story; it is a profound song of resistance and hope.



Here is the historical context behind this African American spiritual.



1. Origins and Authorship

Like most traditional spirituals, the specific author is unknown. The song was composed by enslaved African Americans in the pre-Civil War South (likely the first half of the 19th century).



Oral Tradition: It was passed down orally for generations before being written down.



First Publication: One of the earliest known published versions appeared in 1882 in A Collection of Revival Hymns and Plantation Melodies by Marshall W. Taylor.



2. Biblical Parallel and Symbolism

The song is based on the biblical account in the Book of Joshua (Chapter 6), where the Israelites march around the city of Jericho, blow their trumpets, and the city walls miraculously collapse, allowing them to conquer the city.



For enslaved people, this story held a specific, double meaning:



Joshua: Represented a strong leader who could guide his people to freedom.



Jericho: Represented the system of slavery and the bondage of the American South.



The Walls: Represented the physical and legal barriers preventing their liberty.



"Tumbling Down": A coded expression of hope that the institution of slavery would eventually collapse, just as the biblical walls did.



3. A "Jubilee" Song

Spirituals are generally divided into "Sorrow Songs" (slow, melancholic tracks like Nobody Knows the Trouble I've Seen) and "Jubilees" (upbeat, rhythmic songs).



"Joshua Fit the Battle of Jericho" is a classic Jubilee.



Rhythm: It features a driving, syncopated rhythm that suggests marching and energy.



Tone: Unlike sorrow songs which focus on suffering, this song focuses on victory and the power of God to intervene in human history to crush oppression.



4. Linguistic Note: "Fit" vs. "Fought"

The use of the word "fit" instead of "fought" is a preservation of the African American Vernacular English (AAVE) spoken by the enslaved people who composed the song. While some modern hymnals "correct" this to "fought," historical purists and choral directors often retain "fit" to honor the dialect and the authenticity of the song's original creators.



5. Legacy in the Civil Rights Movement

Because the lyrics focus on tearing down walls of oppression, the song saw a major resurgence during the Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 60s. The "walls" were reinterpreted to represent Jim Crow laws and segregation.



Famous renditions by artists like Paul Robeson and Mahalia Jackson transformed it into a modern anthem for social justice, proving its enduring power to inspire those facing insurmountable odds.
